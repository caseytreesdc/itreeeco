ANALYSIS 2015

Trees provide enormous benefits to human health and the environment. Those natural woody plants standing tall on our streets, in our parks and in our yards filter air pollution, remove carbon dioxide, produce oxygen, absorb stormwater, provide shade, reduce human stress and increase concentration. The data for this report comes from i-Tree, which is a suite of tools from the USDA Forest Service that is designed to use field data from tree inventories along with local hourly air pollution and meteorological data to reveal the structure, function and value of urban forests.

Carbon

Urban trees mitigate climate change by sequestering and storing atmospheric carbon. Trees also provide shade to buildings and homes, altering energy use, lowering carbon dioxide emissions from power plants and changing how energy is used.

D.C.’s urban forest stores 649,000 tons of carbon which is equivalent to 2,381,830 tons of Carbon Dioxide every year.

That’s equivalent to removing 506,772 vehicles from the road. If you were to line those vehicles up end to end they would stretch from Boston to Miami.

Stormwater Runoff

Washington, D.C. is nestled between the Potomac and Anacostia Rivers. On average, 486 million gallons of water is withdrawn daily in the Washington area for water supply.

Each year D.C.’s trees filter over 330 million gallons of water.

The aquatic habitat and water quality of our rivers remain vulnerable to all types of pollutants especially those coming from stormwater runoff.

5 million people live within the Potomac watershed, which includes all of the land area where water drains towards the mouth of the Potomac.


When rainwater falls it can either land on pavement where it picks up pollutants, runs into our sewer system and out into our rivers OR it can land on soil where trees and plants filter that water before it deposits into groundwater reserves.

Tree roots are an important part of that equation — they keep soils porous allowing for water to filter through layers of earth. This process allows toxins, nutrients, sediment and other substances to be filtered out of the water.


Each year DC’s trees filter 44,274,580 cubic feet of water. That's equal to about 500 olympic size swimming pools.

Different species in the district have varying effects on avoided runoff. In D.C., tulip and beech trees account for the highest cubic feet of avoided run off — while crape myrtles account for the least by over 1 million cubic feet. It's important to note that there are twice as many crape myrtles as american elms but contain almost half the leaf area. That means these smaller trees are doing less of the important work we need them to do.

Air Quality

Poor air quality is a common problem in many urban areas that can lead to an increase in chronic respiratory diseases such as asthma. The urban forest can help improve air quality by reducing air temperature and directly removing pollutants from the air.

Every year D.C.'s trees remove 870,000 pounds of pollutants from the air.

15.5 percent of D.C. residents have asthma, which is above the national average of 13.5 percent

Air Pollutants

Carbon Monoxide

A highly toxic gas present in the exhaust of internal-combustion engines.

Nitrogen Dioxide

An irritating gas produced as a result of fossil fuel combustion processes.

Sulfur Dioxide

Produced through the burning of fossil fuels by power plants and other industrial facilities.

Particulate Matter

Very small fragments of solid materials or liquid droplets suspended in air.

Ground Level Ozone

a colorless and highly irritating gas that forms just above the earth's surface.

Trees Filter Harmful Pollutants

Molecularly

Gaseous pollutants are taken out of the air by trees primarily through leaf stomata. Stomata are small “windows” on green leaves, which let carbon dioxide in and oxygen out. These stomata also accept gaseous pollutants in small amounts where it diffuses into spaces between leaf cells. Once inside the leaf, gases diffuse into intercellular spaces and may be absorbed by water films to form acids or react with inner-leaf surfaces.

Physically

Airborne particles, or particulate matter, is removed (at least temporarily) from the air by mechanical interception, primarily on leaf surfaces. While these particles float around in the air, their microscopic size enables them to be breathed deep into our lungs, potentially causing serious health problems or just a temporary irritation.

Conclusion

Plant trees where the people are. Urban trees can make our air healthier and our cities more verdant and livable. If planted in the right spot, trees are one of the single best investments cities can make in improving human health.

Tree Species Composition in D.C.

The urban forest of Washington, D.C. has an estimated 2,431,000 trees. The three most common species are American beech (15.9 percent), boxelder (6.1 percent), and tulip tree (4.6 percent).

Important Species

Many tree benefits equate directly to healthy leaf surface area. In Washington, D.C. the Tulip tree, American beech, and American elm are the most dominant species in terms of leaf area.

iTree takes both the leaf area and percent population into account when determining the Importance Value of each species.

D.C.'s Most 'Important' Trees
